February Update

January was my first full month with the Anytone 878. I feel like I’ve got the basics down now — codeplugs make sense, BrandMeister isn’t intimidating anymore, and the hotspot behaves mostly the way I expect it to. Still a beginner, but not lost.

February has moved fast, but it also feels like I’ve learned so much that it’s been longer than it actually has. Less structured, more exploring. I kept finding new rabbit holes and trying to figure out what parts of this hobby I actually care about most.

At the beginning of the month, I had my first ISS reception. I used an ISS tracker app and caught an afternoon pass that was almost straight overhead — about 80° elevation. I heard stations from Iowa, Ohio, and Connecticut coming through the International Space Station.

That was one of those moments where radio stops being abstract. You’re standing there with a handheld, and you’re hearing signals relayed through something orbiting the Earth at 17,000 mph. Having to accommodate for Doppler shift during the 10-minute pass was probably the coolest part. It makes physics feel immediate.

Programming + Radio

One thing I’ve wanted to do for several years is have an actual reason to buy and work with a Raspberry Pi. With my DMR hotspot running Pi-Star, I finally have an in. I’ve learned some very rudimentary Linux command line skills (git, ssh, nano, sudo, editing config files, etc.), which has been very satisfying.

I wrote a Python script that takes the Pi-Star log files, parses them, converts the data to JSON, and automatically pushes it to my site using the GitHub API. The site itself is hosted on GitHub Pages.

Originally, I tried using Cloudflare Workers, but that turned out to be unnecessary complexity for what I’m doing. I simplified it since my goal is basically a static site and a few focused Python scripts.

I did have ChatGPT generate the JavaScript for displaying the data and help outline the Python script. It’s been a useful guide, especially when I make sure I understand any lines of code I don’t recognize. At the same time, I’m not trying to memorize syntax (hopeless). I care much more about understanding the concepts. That’s where I want the effort to go. I also wrote a lot of additional Python myself, and that part matters to me.

Now I’ve got a live dataset of my own activity updating automatically. That opens the door to experimenting — adding geographic info, visualizing patterns, maybe looking at time-of-day trends. Data analysis is another area I’m trying to explore, so this feels like a natural extension.

It’s small, but it feels like building infrastructure instead of just consuming technology.

Operating

I’ve made a few QSOs — mostly on TG3100 (USA) and TG91 (Worldwide). Not a ton.

If I’m honest, when I’m tired I don’t feel like talking. And most days, the only time I really have available to talk is when I’m tired.

Still, I’m aiming for somewhere between 100–300 QSOs this year. That’s enough to build real experience without turning it into a numbers game.

I’m also going to try to make time for a couple of weekly nets so I can become more active in the local ham scene.

It’s easy for me to retreat into theory and projects. But transmitting matters. This is a communication hobby. I don’t want to be the guy who understands antennas but never keys up.

What’s Actually Driving This

Since I decided to pick up this hobby, I’ve realized what’s really pulling me into amateur radio isn’t just the gear or even the contacts. It’s the science.

I want to understand what’s happening underneath it all — not just “this works,” but why it works.

I’ve been influenced a lot by Daniel and Kelly’s Extraordinary Universe and especially by Remembrance of Earth's Past by Cixin Liu. That trilogy permanently changed how I think about physics and long-term human progress. It made electromagnetic theory feel important — even tied to the future of the human race. And any small bit of knowledge I can acquire contributes, however tiny.

Right now I’m about halfway through The Radio Theory Handbook by Ron Bertrand (VK2DQ). It’s doing exactly what I need — rebuilding fundamentals before jumping into heavier material.

The long-term goal is to really understand Maxwell’s equations. Not just recognize them — actually understand what they mean. I had 2–3 years of calculus in high school. I’m rusty, but I don’t think it’s beyond reach if I approach it seriously.

After finishing The Radio Theory Handbook and brushing up on calculus, I plan to work through Introduction to Electrodynamics by David J. Griffiths.

I’m realistic. I’m not trying to become a professional physicist. But I want depth. Even undergraduate-level mastery of this material would be meaningful.

Broad Direction

I don’t want this to turn into ten unrelated hobbies hiding under the word “radio.” I’m trying to let it grow, but in a way that actually builds on itself.

Right now, that looks like this.

I want to operate more. I’m aiming for somewhere between 100–300 QSOs this year. Not because the number matters that much, but because I can already see how easy it is for me to retreat into theory and projects instead of actually getting on the air. If I’m tired, I don’t feel like talking. That’s real. But if I never push past that, I won’t grow as an operator.

I also plan to get my General and Amateur Extra this year. But I don’t want to brute-force the question pool and move on. I’d rather understand the material first and let the exams reflect that. That’s why I’m working through fundamentals and brushing up on calculus before diving into heavier material.

HF is clearly in my future. I’ve already been thinking through 40 meters, antenna height (around 60 feet if I can manage it), inverted-V options, and what kind of pattern I’d want if I’m trying to work Europe. I don’t want a yard full of random wires, and I don’t want to install something I don’t understand. When I put up an HF antenna, I want to know what it’s doing and why.

On the programming side, I want to keep building small systems around my station. The automated Pi-Star logging was a good start. I’d like to add geographic info, maybe visualize propagation patterns, and keep refining the site so it feels clean and intentional. Nothing flashy — just steady improvement.

Underneath all of this is the real reason I’m here: physics. I want Maxwell’s equations to make sense someday. I’m not trying to become a professional physicist. But I do want to understand electromagnetic theory at a level that feels solid and earned. Amateur radio feels like a practical doorway into that world.

There are a lot of possible side paths — digital modes, SDR, meteor scatter, antenna modeling, hardware builds. I’ll probably touch some of them. But I don’t need to chase everything at once.

This is going to take years. That’s fine.

Decided on the Anytone AT-D878, which supports both analog and DMR and has local repeaters in range. Since HF isn’t available with a Technician license, DMR felt like a practical way to explore DX without the cost and complexity of HF infrastructure.

An HT can also work satellites, and that alone will eventually make the hobby worthwhile.

I kept expectations low for FM range since I’m not near major repeaters, but again, DMR serves as a backup path. Purchased the radio and will wait to open it until licensed / Christmas.

First repeater heard: 442.9 MHz Alpine, NJ (~10–15 miles).

Listening to my first net on Christmas night was tremendously exciting.

January 31 – February 1: Things Clicking Into Place

The last couple of days brought a lot of mental clarity, mostly from spending more time listening to active repeaters and working through an expanded codeplug.

The biggest breakthrough was understanding Digital Monitor on the AT878. Being able to listen to both time slots or all talkgroups on a single slot makes a DMR repeater feel much more like analog. You still need to select the correct talkgroup before transmitting, but Digital Monitor is invaluable for getting a sense of what’s actually happening on a repeater.

I also got a clearer picture of NEDECN. As a DMR-MARC network, it’s related to but separate from BrandMeister. Some BrandMeister talkgroups are bridged, but they’re not the same system. Being in range of the Southborough repeater gives me reliable access to NEDECN, which is proving useful.

Another issue finally made sense: changing talkgroups on the Pi-Star hotspot. You have to wait until the system is idle before transmitting. On something like TG 91 Worldwide, that’s easier said than done, but once I started listening for breaks, it became manageable.

With these pieces in place, a real monitoring strategy emerged. I can use Digital Monitor on the Southborough repeater to hear all DMR activity while keeping an analog wide-area repeater like W1BIM or W1BOS on the other channel, or park a quieter local repeater there while listening to a busy BrandMeister talkgroup.

Overall, day-to-day VHF and DMR operation is starting to make a lot more sense. I also managed a QSO on TG 93 (USA).
