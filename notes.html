Broad Direction

I don’t want this to turn into ten unrelated hobbies hiding under the word “radio.” I’m trying to let it grow, but in a way that actually builds on itself.

I’m aiming for somewhere between 100–300 QSOs this year. The number itself isn’t the point — it’s about operating consistently instead of retreating into theory or projects when I’m tired.

I plan to earn my General and Amateur Extra this year. Not by memorizing the pool and forgetting it, but by actually understanding the material behind it.

HF is clearly ahead. I’ve been thinking through 40 meters, antenna height (around 60 feet if possible), inverted-V options, and what kind of pattern I’d want if I’m trying to work Europe. I don’t want random wire in the yard. When I put something up, I want to understand what it’s doing.

On the programming side, I want to keep building small systems around the station. The Pi-Star logging automation was a good start. I’d like to layer in geographic data, maybe propagation visualization, and keep refining the site so it feels intentional and clean.

Underneath all of this is physics. I want Maxwell’s equations to make sense someday. Amateur radio is the practical doorway into that.

Operate. Study. Build. Keep going.

Winter Storm Reception Test

During a major winter storm that dropped roughly 24 inches of snow, I monitored local repeaters throughout the event. With everything quiet and snow-covered, signals were noticeably clean.

I was able to clearly receive activity from:

  • Whitman – WA1NPO
  • West Bridgewater
  • Bridgewater – W1MV
  • Walpole – K1HRV
  • Cumberland – NB1RI
  • Providence – R1MIX
  • Whitman
  • Portsmouth – W1SYE

This confirmed solid receive coverage across southeastern Massachusetts and into Rhode Island, even under heavy winter conditions. The station performed reliably.
