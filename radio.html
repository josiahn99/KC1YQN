The AT-D878 is a solid first radio. It supports analog FM, DMR, APRS, and GPS, making it easy to explore different parts of the hobby. The features grow with you instead of being outgrown quickly. These are some tips from my experience for anyone getting started.

First Steps Before Programming

Codeplugs can be intimidating, which is why some start with a simple analog radio—totally valid. Here’s a low-pressure way to get your bearings:

  • Just listen: Open the box, set analog mode, lower the squelch, and scan with the up/down arrow. Focus on what’s active and how location/antenna affects reception.
  • Manual programming: Enter a few analog repeaters via the keypad. The interface feels old-school, but quick adjustments without a computer are useful and reinforce TX/RX offsets and PL tones.
  • Get a feel: Don’t worry if the rubber duck is limited. With a Signal Stick, staying vertical and near a window makes a big difference.
Codeplug

Once comfortable scanning and entering repeaters, organize your codeplug. Keep FM repeaters, DMR repeaters, and hotspots separate, since each behaves differently.

  • FM repeaters: Use RepeaterBook to find wide-coverage repeaters for “Tier 1.” Smaller nearby repeaters go in “Tier 2.” Even if mostly quiet, having them ready is handy.
  • DMR repeaters: Correct talkgroups and time slots matter. Digital Monitor helps you hear activity. TG 9 (local) is essential. BrandMeister and NEDECN are separate networks.
  • Hotspots: Provide reliable worldwide access. Changes take effect only when idle, ideal for experimentation without depending on RF propagation.

Technical realizations:

  • Pull repeaters by distance to catch nearby systems.
  • Well-organized zones/scan lists make daily use smoother.
  • Full signal but no audio? Usually a talkgroup/time slot issue.
  • More talkgroups reduce confusion on overlapping repeaters.
Antennas

Indoor operation shows that antenna and placement matter as much as the radio. Everything here is portable—no permanent installs.

Antenna Range (miles) Notes
Rubber Duck 5–10 Simple, always ready
Signal Stick 15–25 Portable, vertical near window, receives satellites
Slim Jim (window) ≈40 Indoor terrestrial, consistent RX/TX

Looking Ahead

Future goals as I get more comfortable:

  • Satellite work: Signal Stick works for receive; eventually plan to try an Arrow antenna for two-way.
  • APRS: Track stations, integrate weather, telemetry.
  • DMR expansion: Experiment with talkgroups, time slots, bridging networks.
  • Portable/mobile setups: Flexible indoor/outdoor use with reliable RX/TX.
  • Refining codeplug: Zones, talkgroups, scan lists—organized for predictability and fun.
