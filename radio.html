Anytone AT-D878UVII Plus

So far, I have no regrets—it was the right choice for me.

For anyone considering this as a first radio, here are a few lessons learned:

Lesson 1: Options matter when coverage is limited.
A $25 Baofeng was tempting, but I’m not near major repeaters. An analog-only handheld could have ended up unused. I wanted a reliable path to QSOs, even if that meant digital modes and a hotspot. A full-featured handheld gave me room to experiment without immediately committing to an HF base station. That said, the Yaesu FT-70 is a popular, more affordable digital option.

Lesson 2: Start with analog before touching DMR.
Analog operation is more intuitive, and getting on the air—scanning bands or listening to local repeaters—is quick. The AT-878 menus are not especially friendly, so Bridgecom Systems programming videos are worth watching. It’s better to know you lack reach than to wonder if a PL tone or offset was set wrong (5 MHz on 70 cm, 0.6 MHz on 2 m).

Lesson 3: DMR isn’t hard, but it isn’t obvious.
After some trial and error, I learned how codeplugs, zones, channels, talkgroups, and scan lists fit together. If you don’t mind spreadsheet-style organization, it’s manageable—and even satisfying.

I’m also out of range of digital repeaters, which made a DMR hotspot necessary. More details are linked here.
